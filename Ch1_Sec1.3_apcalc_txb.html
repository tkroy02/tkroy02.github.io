1. The Idea of a Limit at Infinity

A **limit at infinity** describes the end behavior of a function. It answers the question: "What value does \( f(x) \) approach as \( x \) becomes extremely large (either positive or negative)?"

This is expressed in notation as:

\[ \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L \quad \text{or} \quad \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L \]

where \(L\) is a specific numerical value. If the function approaches a horizontal line, that line is called a **horizontal asymptote**.

2. Horizontal Asymptotes

A horizontal asymptote is a horizontal line \( y = L \) that a function's graph approaches as \( x \to \infty \) or \( x \to -\infty \). A function can have one, two, or no horizontal asymptotes.

3. Finding Limits at Infinity of Rational Functions

For rational functions (fractions of polynomials), the limit at infinity is determined by comparing the **degrees** of the numerator and the denominator.

 

Rule 1: Numerator Degree < Denominator Degree

The limit is always **zero**. The denominator grows faster, pulling the fraction towards zero.

\[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x + 1}{x^2 + 5} = 0 \]

Rule 2: Numerator Degree = Denominator Degree

The limit is the **ratio of the leading coefficients**.

\[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2 - x}{x^2 + 4x} = \frac{3}{1} = 3 \]

This means there is a horizontal asymptote at \( y = 3 \).

Rule 3: Numerator Degree > Denominator Degree

The limit **does not exist** (DNE). The numerator grows faster, causing the function to go to \( \infty \) or \( -\infty \).

\[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3 + 2}{x^2 - 1} = \infty \]

Check Yourself

Find \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{6x^2 - 3x + 1}{2x^2 + 5x - 4}\).

The degrees of the numerator and denominator are both 2. The limit is the ratio of the leading coefficients:

\[ \frac{6}{2} = 3 \]

Find \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{4x}{x^3 + 1}\).

The degree of the numerator (1) is less than the degree of the denominator (3). The limit is 0.
