1. The Tangent Line Problem

In algebra, we learned how to find the slope of a line that passes through two distinct points. In calculus, we are often interested in the slope of a curve at a single point. This is the tangent line problem.

The tangent line to a curve at a point is a line that "just touches" the curve at that point. Its slope gives us the instantaneous rate of change of the function at that precise moment.

2. The Secant Line and the Limit

Since we can't calculate the slope from a single point, we use a trick: we find the slope of a secant line, which is a line passing through two points on the curve.

Let's say we have two points on the curve \( f(x) \): \( (x, f(x)) \) and \( (x + h, f(x + h)) \). The slope of the secant line between these two points is given by the formula:

\[ m_{sec} = \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{(x + h) - x} = \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \]

To find the slope of the tangent line, we let the distance between the two points, \( h \), approach zero. This brings the secant line closer and closer to becoming the tangent line. We do this by taking the limit.

3. The Definition of the Derivative

The derivative of a function \( f(x) \) is a new function, denoted as \( f'(x) \) (read as "f-prime of x"), that gives the slope of the tangent line at any given point \( x \). It is formally defined using the limit of the secant line slope:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \]

This is the definition of the derivative, also known as the limit definition.

4. Basic Differentiation Rules

Calculating derivatives using the limit definition every time can be tedious. Fortunately, there are several shortcuts called differentiation rules.

The Power Rule is one of the most fundamental. It states that for any real number \( n \), the derivative of \( f(x) = x^n \) is \( f'(x) = nx^{n-1} \). In simple terms, you bring the power to the front and subtract one from the exponent.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1} \]

The Constant Rule states that the derivative of a constant is always zero. This makes sense because a constant function is a horizontal line with a slope of zero.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0 \]

The Constant Multiple Rule states that you can pull a constant out of a derivative operation. This is useful when a function is multiplied by a number.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot f'(x) \]

The Sum and Difference Rules state that you can take the derivative of each term separately and then add or subtract them.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \pm g(x)] = f'(x) \pm g'(x) \]

5. Check Yourself

Use the basic rules of differentiation to find the derivative of the following functions.

Question 1: Find the derivative of \( f(x) = 5x^3 - 4x + 7 \).

Using the sum/difference and power rules, we take the derivative of each term individually:

\( f'(x) = \frac{d}{dx}[5x^3] - \frac{d}{dx}[4x] + \frac{d}{dx}[7] \)

\( f'(x) = (5 \cdot 3x^{3-1}) - (4 \cdot 1x^{1-1}) + 0 \)

\( f'(x) = 15x^2 - 4x^0 \)

Since \( x^0 = 1 \), the final answer is:

\( f'(x) = 15x^2 - 4 \)

Question 2: Find the derivative of \( y = \frac{1}{x^2} \).

First, rewrite the function using a negative exponent:

\[ y = x^{-2} \]

Now, apply the Power Rule:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = -2x^{-2-1} = -2x^{-3} \]

Finally, rewrite the answer with a positive exponent:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{2}{x^3} \]

Question 3: Find the derivative of \( g(x) = \sqrt{x} \).

First, rewrite the square root using a fractional exponent:

\[ g(x) = x^{1/2} \]

Now, apply the Power Rule:

\[ g'(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^{\frac{1}{2}-1} = \frac{1}{2}x^{-\frac{1}{2}} \]

Finally, rewrite the answer using a radical:

\[ g'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} \]

Question 4: Find the derivative of \( f(t) = 3t^4 - 2t^3 + 5t - 10 \).

Using the sum/difference and power rules, we differentiate each term:

\[ f'(t) = \frac{d}{dt}[3t^4] - \frac{d}{dt}[2t^3] + \frac{d}{dt}[5t] - \frac{d}{dt}[10] \] \[ f'(t) = 12t^3 - 6t^2 + 5 \]
